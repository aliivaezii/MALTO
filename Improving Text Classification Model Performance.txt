Advanced Optimization Architectures for Extreme Multi-Class Language Model Attribution
The Topography of the Classification Challenge
The attribution of authorship in the contemporary era of generative artificial intelligence has evolved from a simple binary classification problem—distinguishing human writers from machine generators—into a highly complex, multi-dimensional forensic analysis. The current classification task demands the categorization of textual inputs into six distinct classes: Human, DeepSeek, Grok, Claude, Gemini, and ChatGPT.1This problem space is characterized by extreme distributional skew, presenting a severe 19:1 class imbalance within the provided 2,400-sample training corpus.1The majority class, human-written text, dominates the distribution with 1,520 samples (63.3%), while minority target classes, specifically DeepSeek and Claude, are represented by a mere 80 samples (3.3%) each.1
The primary evaluation constraint governing this optimization task is the Macro F1 Score.1Unlike accuracy or micro-averaged metrics, the Macro F1 Score calculates the unweighted arithmetic mean of the individual F1 scores for each class, thereby treating the 80-sample DeepSeek class with the exact same mathematical importance as the 1,520-sample human class.2This metric structure severely penalizes any model that achieves high overall accuracy by collapsing its predictions into the majority class, necessitating a modeling approach that achieves high recall and precision across the extreme long tail of the distribution.2
The current solution pipeline demonstrates sophisticated baseline engineering, achieving a 0.9591 Out-Of-Fold (OOF) cross-validation score and a 0.92170 Public Leaderboard score.1This pipeline leverages a DeBERTa-v3-base transformer architecture, addresses the class imbalance using Focal Loss with a focusing parameter (  ) of 2.0, and incorporates an ensemble of classical machine learning models trained on 46 handcrafted stylometric features alongside a high-dimensional 100,000-feature TF-IDF matrix.1Furthermore, the pipeline utilizes pseudo-labeling on the 600-sample test set and post-hoc temperature scaling for probability calibration.1
Despite these advanced implementations, a critical optimization bottleneck persists regarding the algorithmic confusion between the DeepSeek and Grok generative models.1Resolving this specific inter-class confusion, while simultaneously elevating the performance on all minority classes under the strict prohibition of external datasets and pre-trained AI detectors, requires a fundamental restructuring of the loss landscape, the regularization protocols, and the semantic feature extraction methodologies.1
Deconstructing Generative Fingerprints and Stylometric Topologies
The inability of the current ensemble to cleanly partition the latent space between DeepSeek and Grok indicates that the existing 46 handcrafted features and the standard DeBERTa-v3 embeddings are failing to capture the nuanced, model-specific artifacts embedded within the text. Large Language Models do not generate text uniformly; their outputs are highly deterministic reflections of their underlying architectures, their instruction-tuning paradigms, and their Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) guardrails.8
The Human Baseline Versus Algorithmic Rigidity
An analysis of the raw training text reveals stark morphological and structural deviations between human and machine-generated content. Human texts within the training corpus are characterized by high orthographic variance and structural noise.1These texts consistently display typographical errors, such as transpositions ("ilves" for lives, "citizns" for citizens, "sohuld" for should), duplication errors ("votingg", "attention", "eletcors"), and inconsistent punctuation spacing ("person?.Let", "park? lets").1Furthermore, the human samples frequently exhibit a highly subjective, emotional narrative tone, often adopting a persuasive essay format directed at specific audiences (eg, "Dear state senator") and referencing localized context or prompt-specific constraints (eg, "as it mentions in source 3").1
Conversely, machine-generated texts lack this orthographic entropy. For example, the text instances associated with Claude exhibit a rigid, formulaic macro-structure. The analysis of these samples reveals a highly repetitive paragraph template where a primary assertion is consistently followed by a pivot utilizing the conjunctive adverb "However," which is then followed by a telegraphic, verb-deficient fragment (eg, "However, urgent action necessary," "However, habitat protection prevents loss").1This mechanical repetition of transition structures represents a highly exploitable algorithmic fingerprint.10
The DeepSeek and Grok Dichotomy
The central bottleneck—distinguishing DeepSeek from Grok—requires an understanding of their divergent generative postures. DeepSeek models, particularly those utilizing reasoning-focused architectures, tend to produce highly formal, factual, and straightforward text.12Their syntactic structure relies on dense, complex sentences that prioritize logical flow, often manifesting as step-by-step procedural reasoning.1A training sample associated with DeepSeek demonstrates this extreme conciseness and objectivity, encapsulating a complex astronomical process within a single, perfectly punctuated compound-complex sentence utilizing highly domain-specific vocabulary ("nebula," "white dwarf," "neutron star").1
Grok, developed with a fundamentally different interaction philosophy, is designed to be conversational, witty, and occasionally provocative.12This translates into a distinct stylometric profile characterized by variable sentence lengths, the inclusion of natural language pauses or conversational fillers, and a higher reliance on dynamic punctuation (such as exclamation marks) to convey enthusiasm or humor.12Furthermore, in coding or structured formatting tasks, Grok's outputs often exhibit different structural alignments or markdown idiosyncrasies compared to the highly optimized, logical formatting generated by DeepSeek.14

	Advanced Feature Engineering for Algorithmic Detection
To resolve the classification bottleneck without violating the prohibition on external data, the current 46-feature set must be mathematically expanded to capture the specific phenomena outlined above.1Standard readability indices (such as Flesch-Kincaid) evaluate superficial complexity but fail to capture the algorithmic probability distributions underlying LLM generation.16
The feature engineering pipeline must integrate Part-of-Speech (POS) dependency tracking. By utilizing a local NLP library to extract POS bigrams and unigrams of function words, the model can detect the divergent syntactic pairings used by different LLMs.10DeepSeek's formal text relies heavily on specific noun-adjective pairings and complex prepositions, whereas Grok utilizes more pronoun-verb bigrams reflecting its conversational persona. Research indicates that integrating these specific POS frequencies into a multidimensional scaling framework can achieve near-perfect discrimination between modern LLMs.10
Furthermore, the current utilization of a 100,000-dimensional TF-IDF matrix is ​​mathematically sub-optimal for a dataset containing only 2,400 training samples.1This extreme feature-to-sample ratio guarantees the curse of dimensionality, leading to severe overfitting within the classical meta-learners (LinearSVC and Logistic Regression).17The TF-IDF extraction must be modified from word-level tokens to character-level n-grams (ranging from 3 to 5 characters).8Character n-grams are highly effective at capturing the sub-word morphological artifacts and spacing anomalies that differentiate specific LLM tokenizers.8Subsequently, this high-dimensional matrix must be compressed using Truncated Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) to project the sparse matrix into a dense vector space of 100 to 300 principal components, thereby preserving the latent semantic correlations without overwhelming the classification algorithms.17
Finally, the pipeline must calculate localized pseudo-perplexity and burstiness metrics. While true perplexity calculations require the original autoregressive weights of the target models, pseudo-perplexity can be estimated by utilizing the currently loaded DeBERTa-v3-base model to extract the log-likelihood scores of the sequences.16Human texts exhibit high burstiness, characterized by alternating periods of high and low sentence complexity.16In contrast, algorithmic generators, particularly those optimized for logical reasoning like DeepSeek, maintain a highly consistent, low-burstiness syntactic rhythm that serves as a powerful discriminatory feature.16
Architectural Augmentation: The Integration of ModernBERT
The existing pipeline relies entirely on DeBERTa-v3-baseas the singular transformer backbone.1DeBERTa-v3 represents a formidable architecture for Natural Language Inference (NLI) and classification due to its disentangled attention mechanism and Replaced Token Detection (RTD) pre-training objective.20However, relying on a single encoder topology limits the diversity of the latent representations fed into the final ensemble. To optimize the Macro F1 score and better differentiate between DeepSeek and Grok, the architecture must incorporate a parallel processing track utilizing ModernBERT-base.
ModernBERT is a modernized bidirectional encoder incorporating recent architectural advancements, including Rotary Positional Embeddings (RoPE), alternating local-global attention patterns, and hardware-optimized unpadding mechanisms.23Operating with 149 million parameters, it is designed for extreme computational efficiency and long-context comprehension.23
The strategic integration of ModernBERT addresses several vulnerabilities inherent in the current DeBERTa-centric approach. First, ModernBERT was pre-trained on a massive 2-trillion token mixture containing both natural language and computer code.23Because the differentiation between DeepSeek and Grok frequently hinges on how they format structured data, lists, or markdown—which share syntactic and structural similarities with programming languages—ModernBERT possesses a superior intrinsic sensitivity to these structural artifacts.23Empirical benchmarks demonstrate that encoder models trained on code-heavy mixtures significantly outperform standard NLP encoders on tasks requiring structural comprehension.26
Second, ModernBERT's unpadding and Flash Attention implementations make it significantly faster and more memory-efficient than DeBERTa-v3.20This computational efficiency allows for the expansion of the effective batch size or an increase in gradient accumulation steps without exceeding hardware constraints.23In the context of extreme class imbalance, larger effective batch sizes are crucial for stabilizing the gradient updates corresponding to the 80-sample minority classes.1
By extracting the `` token embeddings from both DeBERTa-v3 and ModernBERT and concatenating these representations, the ensemble benefits from highly uncorrelated feature spaces.30DeBERTa-v3 captures the deep semantic dependencies and masked token anomalies, while ModernBERT captures the structural formatting, markdown idiosyncrasies, and long-range syntactic coherence, providing the downstream classifiers with a significantly enriched topological map of the text.30

	Loss Landscape Topography for Extreme Class Imbalance
The current pipeline attempts to mitigate the 19:1 class imbalance utilizing Focal Loss configured with a focusing parameter of  , combined with static class weights.1While Focal Loss is a standard approach for handling imbalance, its application to LLM authorship attribution—specifically when optimizing for the Macro F1 metric—introduces severe mathematical vulnerabilities that suppress minority class recall.34
The Mathematical Vulnerability of Focal Loss
Focal Loss was engineered for dense object detection in computer vision, a domain where the vast majority of "background" samples are easy to classify, and the model only needs to focus on a few "hard" positive samples.35It achieves this by applying a modulating factor  to the cross-entropy loss, aggressively down-weighting the gradients of well-classified examples.35
However, the boundary between text generated by Grok and text generated by ChatGPT is not an "easy negative" versus a "hard positive"; it is a highly ambiguous, overlapping semantic boundary heavily polluted by noise.36When applied to this dataset, the Focal Loss modulating factor forces the optimizer to disproportionately penalize the model for stylistically ambiguous human texts that happen to resemble AI generation.36Instead of learning the true underlying syntactic distribution of the 80 DeepSeek samples, the decision boundary warps erratically around these noisy outliers.36Empirical studies on medical and text classification datasets confirm that under conditions of moderate to severe label ambiguity, Focal Loss amplifies noise, degrades precision, and ultimately suppresses the Macro F1 score.34
Transitioning to Logit Adjustment (LA) Loss
To mathematically align the training objective with the Macro F1 metric in a severely imbalanced, noisy dataset, the pipeline must transition from Focal Loss to Logit Adjustment (LA) Loss.7

Label-Distribution Aware Margin (LDAM) Loss
An alternative or complementary objective function is the Label-Distribution Aware Margin (LDAM) Loss.35LDAM dictates that the margin enforced for a specific class should be inversely proportional to the square root of its sample size (  ).38
For the MALTO dataset, this enforces a drastic margin scaling:
* Human Class (  ): Minimal margin constraint, allowing the model flexibility in defining the broad human distribution.
* DeepSeek/Claude Classes (  ): Maximum margin constraint, forcing the model to carve out a highly distinct and protected region in the latent space for these rare signatures.
To maximize the efficacy of LDAM, it must be paired with a Deferred Reweighting (DRW) schedule.38Under DRW, the model trains with standard cross-entropy for the initial epochs, allowing the transformer to learn robust, generalized feature manifolds. In the final stages of training, the inverse-frequency weights and LDAM margins are activated, forcing the model to separate the minority classes only after the foundational semantic representations have converged.37
Weight-Space Regularization: Implementing Adversarial Weight Perturbation (AWP)
Training a 140-million parameter model like DeBERTa-v3 on a highly constrained dataset of 2,400 samples—where the target classes contain only 80 instances—creates an extreme risk of robust overfitting.1Without stringent regularization, the transformer will rapidly memorize the specific lexical tokens of the 80 DeepSeek training samples rather than learning the generalized syntactic patterns required to evaluate the 600 unseen test samples.
While the current architecture employs Layer-wise Learning Rate Decay (LLRD) to mitigate catastrophic forgetting1, achieving state-of-the-art performance requires flattening the loss landscape through Adversarial Weight Perturbation (AWP).49
The Mechanics of Flat Minima Optimization
Standard adversarial training techniques, such as the Fast Gradient Method (FGM) or Projected Gradient Descent (PGD), perturb the input embeddings to increase robustness against noisy data.51AWP, conversely, injects adversarial noise directly into the model weights during the optimization cycle.50
AWP operates by formulating training as a double-maximization problem to seek "flat minima" in the loss landscape.50During each training step:
   1. The standard loss is computed using the current network weights.
   2. The gradients of the loss with respect to the weights are calculated.
   3. A scaled perturbation  is temporarily added to the weights in the exact direction that maximizes the loss (ie, the worst-case weight perturbation that makes the model perform as poorly as possible).50
   4. The final optimization step is performed by minimizing the loss computed on these adversarially perturbed weights.50
By forcing the optimizer to locate a region in the weight space where the loss remains consistently low even if the weights are slightly shifted, AWP prevents the model from settling into sharp, narrow minima that perfectly classify the 80 training samples but fail catastrophically on unseen validation data.50

Distribution-Aware Soft Pseudo-Labeling
The current pipeline leverages pseudo-labeling by identifying high-confidence predictions on the 600-sample test set and injecting them back into the training data.1In scenarios characterized by extreme, long-tailed class imbalance, executing standard "hard" pseudo-labeling acts as a destructive feedback loop.64
If the base model is inherently biased toward the majority class (Human), it will naturally produce very few high-confidence predictions for the minority classes (DeepSeek, Claude) on the test set. Consequently, the injected pseudo-labeled data will consist almost entirely of Human, ChatGPT, or Gemini samples, violently exacerbating the initial 19:1 imbalance and resulting in severe confirmation bias during the retraining phase.7
To safely extract signals from the unlabeled test data, the pipeline must implement Distribution-Aware Soft Pseudo-Labeling.66
         1. Ensemble Distillation via Soft Labels: Instead of forcing the test samples into discrete, hard classes (eg, assigning a definitive "1" for DeepSeek and "0" for everything else), the targets must be defined as the raw probability distribution (the softmax output) generated by the Level 1 ensemble.67Training the model against these soft labels forces it to learn the uncertainty and the inter-class relationships .67For example, the model learns that a specific test sample possesses a 60% probability of being Grok and a 30% probability of being DeepSeek, effectively transferring the nuanced decision boundaries of the ensemble back into the base models without enforcing artificial binary confidence.67
         2. Probability Trimming: To prevent the model from learning random noise generated by the ensemble, a strict lower-bound confidence trim must be applied to the soft labels. If any probability within the soft label vector falls below a threshold (eg, 0.10), it is explicitly trimmed to 0.0.67This purges noisy, unconfident gradients while preserving the critical secondary class relationships that differentiate similar LLMs.
         3. Class-Balanced Selection for Hard Labels: If architectural constraints require the use of hard pseudo-labels, the selection mechanism must absolutely abandon the "top N highest confidence overall" approach. Instead, it must utilize a class-wise quota system, selecting the "top K highest confidence" predictions per class .65By forcing the algorithm to select exactly equal numbers of pseudo-labeled samples for Human, DeepSeek, and Grok (even if the absolute confidence for the DeepSeek samples is lower than the Human samples), the dataset is dynamically balanced in the second stage of training, directly combating the initial 19:1 ratio.69
Navigating the Macro F1 Surface: Decision Threshold Optimization
The ultimate objective is the maximization of the Macro F1 score.1Relying on the standard  function—which simply predicts the class with the highest softmax probability—is mathematically sub-optimal for this metric.5The Macro F1 score surface is highly non-convex, and in imbalanced datasets, the optimal decision boundary for maximizing F1 on rare classes diverges significantly from maximum likelihood estimation.5
Class-Wise Decision Threshold Tuning
The continuous probability distributions emitted by the final ensemble must be post-processed using an iterative threshold tuning algorithm.70
         1. Probability Calibration: Threshold tuning requires strictly calibrated probabilities. While the current pipeline utilizes post-hoc temperature scaling1, applying a single global temperature across all classes is insufficient. The probabilities must be calibrated on a per-class basis utilizing Platt Scaling or Isotonic Regression on a held-out validation set to ensure the softmax outputs accurately reflect the true likelihood of correctness for each specific generator.1
         2. Iterative Grid Search: Utilizing the Out-Of-Fold (OOF) predictions generated during the 5-fold cross-validation process, the pipeline must implement an optimization search (such as a Nelder-Mead algorithm or an exhaustive grid search) to discover the unique, optimal probability thresholds for each of the six classes that maximize the global Macro F1 score.1
         3. Threshold Asymmetry: The optimization algorithm will naturally discover an asymmetric threshold distribution. To maximize Macro F1, the threshold required to predict the majority class (Human) must be significantly elevated (eg, demanding  confidence to classify a text as Human). Conversely, the thresholds for the minority classes (DeepSeek and Claude) must be drastically lowered (eg, acceptance  confidence to classify a text as DeepSeek).34Because a false negative in the DeepSeek class damages the unweighted Macro F1 score far more severely than a false positive in the Human class, the model must be biased toward predicting the rare classes when uncertainty exists.2
         4. Sequential Tie-Breaking: Lowering multiple thresholds inevitably results in collisions, where a single sample clears the optimized threshold for more than one class (eg, clearing both the 0.20 threshold for DeepSeek and the 0.30 threshold for Grok). The pipeline must implement a sequential tie-breaking mechanism that explicitly favors the class with the lower prior distribution in the training data.2
Stacked Generalization and Meta-Learning Architecture
The current architecture utilizes a blending ensemble incorporating DeBERTa-v3, LinearSVC, Logistic Regression, XGBoost, and LightGBM.1To maximize the synergistic effects of the newly proposed features and the transformer embeddings, the ensemble must be formalized into a rigorous stacked generalization architecture.
Level 1: Basic Predictor Diversity
The strength of an ensemble is directly proportional to the lack of correlation between the errors of its base predictors.1Level 1 must generate highly diverse probability vectors:
            * Transformer Track A: DeBERTa-v3-base , fine-tuned utilizing Logit Adjustment Loss and Adversarial Weight Perturbation (AWP), optimized for capturing deep semantic relationships and Replaced Token Detection anomalies.20
            * Transformer Track B: ModernBERT-base , fine-tuned utilizing Label-Distribution Aware Margin (LDAM) Loss and DRW, optimized for capturing long-range structural dependencies, markdown artifacts, and coding-style formatting inherent to Grok and DeepSeek.23
            * Classical ML Track: Gradient Boosting frameworks (LightGBM/XGBoost) trained exclusively on the expanded stylometric feature set (POS bigrams, structural dependency depth), the truncated SVD of the character-level n-gram matrix, and the local pseudo-perplexity scores.1
Level 2: The Meta-Learner Optimization
The output probability distributions from the Level 1 predictors (  ) are concatenated to form the input space for the Level 2 meta-learner.77
Because the Level 1 outputs are highly correlated probabilities, utilizing another tree-based model (like XGBoost) at Level 2 invites severe overfitting to the OOF predictions.1Instead, the meta-learner must be a highly regularized linear model, such as a Ridge Classifier (utilizing an L2 penalty), or a shallow Multilayer Perceptron (MLP) employing heavy Dropout.77
The primary function of the meta-learner is not to re-evaluate the text, but to mathematically learn the correlation of errors between the base models. For instance, the meta-learner will computationally recognize that if ModernBERT predicts Grok (due to formatting detection), but DeBERTa-v3 predicts DeepSeek (due to semantic detection), the presence of high punctuation entropy (fed from the LightGBM stylometric track) should reliably tip the final probability calculation toward Grok.77
Synthesis of Optimization Directives
To successfully shatter the 0.9591 Out-Of-Fold plateau and systematically resolve the DeepSeek versus Grok classification bottleneck, the current pipeline must transition away from generalized classification paradigms and adopt a mathematically rigorous framework designed specifically for extreme, long-tailed NLP attribution.1
The integration of Logit Adjustment Loss will directly align the gradient descent trajectory with the Macro F1 objective by mathematically excising the 19:1 base rate bias, allowing the model to establish stable decision boundaries for the 80-sample minority classes without the noise amplification inherent to Focal Loss.7Simultaneously, deploying Adversarial Weight Perturbation (AWP) in the upper layers of the transformer will enforce flat minimum optimization, critically preventing robust overfitting on the highly constrained training data and ensuring the learned representations generalize to the test distribution.50
Furthermore, augmenting the DeBERTa-v3 backbone with a parallel ModernBERT-base architecture will exploit ModernBERT's code-heavy pre-training and structural awareness, providing the specific latent features required to differentiate the highly logical, chain-of-thought formatting of DeepSeek from the conversational, variable structuring of Grok.23By combining these architectural advancements with Class-Wise Threshold Optimization , Distribution-Aware Soft Pseudo-Labeling , and Character-Level SVD Stylometrics , the resulting stacked generalization pipeline will be uniquely calibrated to isolate the definitive generative fingerprints of each target language model.
